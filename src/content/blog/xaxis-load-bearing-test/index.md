## A project that deleted its most fashionable feature

In the spring of 2026 a Bitcoin protocol project sat on a finished design for the most marketable thing it could have shipped. The feature was agent delegation, a scheme for letting one keyholder grant scoped, revocable authority to an automated agent, written in the same months the entire software industry was reorganizing itself around exactly that idea. The plan existed. The gap analysis existed. Then it was archived, and the archive note states the reason without softening it. The first reason given is that Bitcoin was not load-bearing. The design used secp256k1 signatures and content-addressed identifiers, but the Bitcoin chain contributed nothing the protocol could not have gotten from an Ed25519 keypair and a content-addressed store. The second reason is that no actual integrator had asked for it; the forums and relays and airdrop operators in the room were asking for a spam filter, not an agent orchestrator.

That deletion is more instructive than most things that ship. The project, OrangeCheck, internally OCHK, is a family of six small protocols for doing recognizably civic things on the open internet: proving you are not a bot, encrypting a message to someone, running a weighted poll, timestamping a document, delegating a narrow authority, and making a public commitment. None of them is a payment system. None of them is a chain, a token, or a second layer. What unifies them is not a feature set but a refusal, encoded as a test the project applies to itself before anything is allowed in. The test is the contribution. Everything downstream of it is competent plumbing, and the plumbing is only interesting because the test decided what to plumb.

## The substitution test is the actual invention

The rule reads as one sentence. Before any mechanism is admitted to the family, ask whether it would work identically if every Bitcoin address were swapped for a generic Ed25519 keypair. If the answer is yes, the mechanism does not belong, because Bitcoin is then ornamental and the thing being built is a worse version of credential systems that already exist. The retired delegation feature failed this test, and the failure was the reason it died.

Most of what calls itself a Bitcoin application fails the same test and ships anyway. A wallet that lets you log into a website with your keys is using a signature, and any signature scheme would do; the Bitcoin part is decoration. A loyalty token issued on a sidechain is using Bitcoin as a marketing adjective. The substitution test is uncomfortable precisely because so little survives it. It forces a question the broader cryptocurrency industry has spent fifteen years avoiding: not what can be attached to Bitcoin, but what specifically requires the one property Bitcoin has that nothing else credibly reproduces. The honest answer to that question is a short list, and a project disciplined enough to hold the list is rarer than a project with a long roadmap.

What survives the test is anything that depends on a Bitcoin address proving control without moving funds, on a UTXO having a publicly verifiable age and balance, on a Bitcoin block serving as a neutral clock no one administers, or on the opportunity cost of held coins functioning as a cost an attacker pays and an honest user does not. Those are properties of Bitcoin that a fresh keypair does not have. A keypair is free to generate in unlimited quantity. A coin held still for a month is not. That asymmetry is the entire raw material, and the family is an attempt to build civic primitives out of it and nothing else.

The shape of the project changed once the test was taken seriously, and the change is the second half of the lesson. The earlier version was a single protocol straining to do everything, attestation and delegation and reputation scoring folded into one canonical message format with a free-text scope field and an advisory score. The move that followed was decomposition. Instead of one protocol that did several jobs adequately and made Bitcoin ornamental in the parts it handled least well, the work split into separate protocols, each admitted only after demonstrating that a distinct Bitcoin property was load-bearing in its core concern: holding cost for sybil resistance, balance for voting weight, block time for provenance and revocation ordering, address control for encryption identity. The single broad primitive that worked with other systems became a set of narrow primitives that each failed the substitution test in a different direction, which is the only direction failing it is a good sign. A system that does one thing because Bitcoin is the only way to do it is worth more than a system that does ten things any keypair could do.

## What is scarce here is not the coin but the patience

The foundational protocol, OrangeCheck Attest, is the clearest demonstration of which property is doing the work. The problem it addresses is sybil resistance, the oldest unsolved problem of every open network: how to filter spam and fake accounts without becoming a centralized identity gatekeeper that demands a phone number and a government name. Centralized platforms solve it by collecting personal data. Open protocols, by construction, cannot, which is why Nostr, decentralized forums, airdrop distributions, and on-chain governance all share the same chronic weakness.

The mechanism is almost embarrassingly plain. A user signs a canonical message with their Bitcoin wallet using BIP-322, binding their address to whatever external handles they want to claim, a Nostr key, a domain, a code-hosting username. No transaction is broadcast. No funds move. A verifier takes the address, checks the signature, queries public chain data for the unspent outputs at that address, and computes two numbers: how many satoshis sit there, and how many days the oldest output has sat unspent. The unit that matters is the product, satoshis multiplied by days. A relying party sets a threshold and gates on it.

The economic claim underneath is the part worth taking seriously. Holding, say, one hundred thousand satoshis unspent for thirty days costs roughly seventy dollars in foregone yield and liquidity, a number small enough to be free in practice for a real participant and ruinous for an adversary who needs ten thousand identities, because the adversary must replicate the stake ten thousand times and hold it idle for the whole window. Spending the coins to recycle them into a new identity destroys the proof, since the output age resets. This is proof of stake in the literal, pre-tokenized sense of the phrase: stake denominated in the one asset whose holding cost is real, externally observable, and not issued by the system charging it. No Ed25519 keypair can carry an age or a balance, which is why this passes the test where the delegation feature did not. The scarce thing is not the coin. It is the demonstrated willingness to leave the coin alone, and that willingness is what cannot be faked at scale.

## Six verbs that all read from the same address

The family extends the same logic across five further protocols, each chosen because the load-bearing Bitcoin property is different in each, and each declining to touch the chain beyond what it strictly needs. OC Lock handles confidentiality. An earlier version tried to tie decryption to an on-chain spend using adaptor signatures, and it was discarded for a sharp conceptual reason: Bitcoin should prove identity, not enforce access control. The shipped design binds an X25519 device key to a Bitcoin address with a single BIP-322 signature, then runs ordinary elliptic-curve key agreement and authenticated encryption with AES-256-GCM. Bitcoin proves who you are. The encryption does the rest, off-chain, instantly, with no ten-minute confirmation wait and no transaction fee. The restraint is the design.

OC Vote is the most ambitious member and the one that best exposes the philosophy. It runs stake-weighted polls where a voter's weight is the balance of their unspent outputs at a chosen snapshot block height. Ballots are published to Nostr; the tally is a pure function that any observer with chain data can recompute and confirm offline, with no token to issue, no governance chain to run, and no vote-counting server to trust. Compare this to the dominant tools. Token-based governance requires a token, which is a liquid attack surface anyone can rent on a market. Verifiable election systems require a trusted setup ceremony and an administering authority. OC Vote needs neither, because it borrows weight from an asset that already exists and a clock no one owns. The non-obvious insight is the identification of which Bitcoin property governance actually needs. It is not identity and it is not payment. It is a credible, neutral, time-aware weight, and Bitcoin holdings are the only candidate that is simultaneously permissionless, hard to counterfeit, and priced in genuine opportunity cost.

OC Stamp combines a BIP-322 signature over a document hash with an OpenTimestamps anchor into a Bitcoin block, producing a provenance record that ties authorship to a moment in chain time and verifies later from block headers alone. OC Agent, the rebuilt successor to the deleted feature, grants scoped authority bonded to an OrangeCheck stake and orders revocations against actions by their timestamp in the chain, so that a revocation race resolves by block order rather than by trusting a server's clock. OC Pledge records forward-looking commitments resolved against deterministic public facts. Notably, the entire family uses no covenant opcodes, no CHECKTEMPLATEVERIFY, no script-enforced vaults. The critical path is kept off-chain on purpose, and Bitcoin is touched only for the narrow thing it is uniquely good at. Six verbs, one address, and a consistent answer to the question of what the chain is for.

## The refusals are the part that honors the ethos

It is worth being precise about what the Bitcoin ethos actually is, because the word gets stretched to cover any sentiment a speaker likes. The durable core is a small set of commitments: no trusted third party, self-custody of the asset, censorship resistance, no privileged issuer, and verification rather than trust. Bitcoin earned those properties for money. The interesting move OrangeCheck makes is to ask whether the same properties can be extended past money to the other things a person does in public, and to extend them only where the chain genuinely supplies them rather than asserting them by association.

The refusals are where this becomes legible. The project will not issue a token, because a token reintroduces the thing Bitcoin was built to remove, a privileged instrument whose value depends on the issuer. It will not run a second-layer chain, because that imports a new consensus to trust. It custodies nothing; funds never move and there is no wallet inside the application holding keys. There is no account and no identity collection, because the address is the identity and a signature is the entire enrollment ceremony. Every artifact the system produces is content-addressed, identified by the hash of its own canonical bytes, so that altering any field changes the identifier and invalidates the signature over it. Every artifact is offline-verifiable, meaning a skeptic with chain data and a signature verifier can confirm it without asking the project's servers anything. And where a trust assumption is unavoidable, the stated rule is to name it in the specification rather than bury it.

This is the ethos applied as engineering constraint rather than slogan. The contrast with the surrounding industry is the point. The standard pattern for monetizing a protocol is to issue a token and capture its appreciation, which is precisely the move that fails the no-privileged-issuer test. OrangeCheck's stated revenue intention, where revenue arrives at all, is to settle in Bitcoin or Lightning, never to mint an instrument. The specifications are public under a permissive license; the reference code is small and built on audited libraries with no custom cryptography. A protocol that can be verified from a person's own node, that issues nothing, that holds nothing, and that names its trust anchors is the closest thing to a faithful extension of the original design that the broader space has produced, and it got there by subtraction.

## Stake-weighting is wealth-weighting, and the honest version says so

A sober reading has to hold the strongest objection in view, and the strongest objection is structural rather than technical. Stake weighting is wealth weighting. A poll whose weight function is unspent balance gives the largest holder the loudest voice, which reproduces inside the governance mechanism the exact distribution of holdings that Bitcoin's own critics object to. The project does not hide from this; the voting specification declines to claim it can replace sovereign elections and is explicit that legal elections require accessibility, adversarial audit, and binding to legal identity that a stake-weighted poll cannot provide. That honesty is creditable, but it does not dissolve the limit. There is a real and permanent difference between a tool that measures conviction-weighted-by-stake and a tool that measures one-person-one-vote, and a great deal of civic life depends on the second. OrangeCheck is a credible substrate for the first and an explicit non-candidate for the second, and the distinction will be lost the moment the tool is popular enough to be misapplied.

The same discipline that names this limit names several others, and a critic should read the named ones as the floor of the problem rather than the ceiling. The voting protocol in its current form offers no receipt-freeness; once a secret ballot is revealed, a voter's choice is publicly linkable to their address, which means coercion resistance is absent and a buyer of votes can verify delivery. The poll creator holds the reveal key and can therefore decrypt mid-poll, a position of asymmetric visibility mitigated only by a published signature and a promised future of threshold reveal. The payment-gated mode of the encryption protocol depends on a trusted relay, because doing it trustlessly would require chain mechanisms that are not shippable today. And the entire family rests on elliptic-curve cryptography that is secure only against classical computers; secp256k1 and X25519 both fall to a sufficiently large quantum machine, which is a risk shared with Bitcoin itself but inherited wholesale here.

## The trust anchors that remain are named, not hidden

The strongest version of the project still has trust anchors, and the most useful thing an analyst can do is locate them precisely rather than accept the headline that there are none. Publication runs over Nostr relays, and relays can censor; the mitigation is publishing to several at once, which reduces the risk without eliminating the dependency on relay operators as a class. The enforcement model for commitments is reputation, not slashing. A broken pledge is not liquidated on-chain; it is recorded in an immutable public log and left to cost the breaker their standing. That only bites if reputation is legible and valued by the people who matter to the breaker, which is an assumption about social context, not a property of the protocol, and it is weakest exactly where it would need to be strongest, against a well-funded actor who does not care what a log says about them.

There is also one explicit exception to the no-custody rule, and it is the consumer product that pays users satoshis for holding a Bitcoin-backed identity, which is custodied through a Fedimint federation. A federation is a named, bounded trust assumption rather than a single custodian, which is a defensible engineering choice, but it is custody, and it sits in tension with the family invariant it is exempted from. The right way to read these anchors is not as hypocrisy but as the residue that honesty leaves behind. A project that claimed zero trust would be lying. A project that names its relays, its reveal-key holder, its trusted relay mode, its reputation assumption, and its one custodial exception has told you where to point your skepticism, which is the most a protocol can do for an adversarial reader.

## Correctness is settled; adoption is the wager

The technical evidence is strong in the way that small systems built on audited primitives tend to be strong. The cryptography is conventional and not invented in-house. The specifications ship with cross-implementation test vectors carrying real hashes, so that two independent implementations can be checked for byte-identical behavior. The protocols are minimal enough to read in an afternoon and verify from a personal node. On the axis of does-it-do-what-it-says, the answer appears to be yes, and that is not a small thing in a field where most claims collapse under a careful read.

The open question is the one no specification can answer. Sybil resistance priced in held Bitcoin only works if relying parties adopt it, and adoption is a coordination problem that lives entirely outside the protocol. A spam filter that no forum installs filters nothing. A weighted poll that no community runs weighs nothing. The wager OrangeCheck makes is that a small Bitcoin balance left unspent is the cheapest credible proof-of-personhood the open internet can get without surveillance, and that enough operators will eventually agree to make the proof worth carrying. The protocols are correct. Whether the wager pays is a fact about people, not about code, and it has not been settled.

What the project has already demonstrated, regardless of how the wager resolves, is the discipline. It found the short list of things for which Bitcoin's specific scarcity is irreplaceable, it built only those, and it deleted the feature that would have sold best because the chain was doing no work in it. The ethos was never really about money. It was about removing the trusted party from the things that matter, and money was only the first of those things. A project that extends the removal to identity, voice, provenance, and commitment, and that proves its seriousness by what it refuses to ship, is closer to the original idea than most things wearing the same logo. The hard part was never building on Bitcoin. The hard part was being willing to walk away from everything that did not need it.
